## Decision Logic

The verdict is **Validate first**. The idea has a credible wedge,
plausible moat path, and strong technical fit for the assumed 2-person
team, but one demand assumption is not yet earned: independent PT clinic
owners may not feel documentation pain with physician-level urgency.

- Qualification score: **62/100**
- Defensibility score: **54/100**
- Key gate concern: founder-market fit
- Decision-critical uncertainty: willingness to pay for a PT-specific
  scribe at $150-300 per therapist per month

The decision rule is mechanical. Qualification 62 falls in the
Validate-first band, and the top assumption can be tested in under 2
weeks for about $500 before product build.

## Right To Win

The assumed team is well matched to the product shape: HCI/frontend
quality, AI/ML backend ability, and comfort with product-led distribution.
The product requires a polished clinical workflow, fast AI iteration, and
clear positioning.

The team is not yet well matched to the buyer network. It has no direct
PT clinical credibility, no existing clinic-owner list, and no regulatory
advisor. That gap is not fatal, but it must be closed with 2-3 design
partners before moving from validation to build.

## Market Analysis

The fictional market model estimates about 40,000 US outpatient PT
clinics and a beachhead SAM of about 22,000 independent small clinics.
At $1,800-3,600 per therapist per year, the beachhead can support a
meaningful bootstrapped SaaS business without requiring enterprise
accounts.

The stronger market argument is labor replacement, not software budget.
If a therapist spends 30-60 minutes per day on documentation, the
economic buyer can justify a tool that returns clinical time and reduces
late work. The unresolved question is urgency: clinic owners may accept
documentation as unavoidable overhead unless the pain is framed in
retention, claims, or throughput terms.

## Competitive Landscape

The real substitute is manual typing in the EHR. Horizontal scribes such
as fictional ScribeMD Pro and NotePal prove category demand but are
optimized for physician workflows. PT-specific workflow depth creates the
opening: exercise prescription, SOAP-note structure, therapy CPT coding,
and therapist correction feedback.

The most dangerous competitor is not today's PT-native tool; it is a
horizontal scribe adding a PT module after the category is validated. The
defense is speed into vertical data and workflow embedding. A shallow
PT label would not be enough.

## Revenue Model

The pricing hypothesis is $150-300 per therapist per month, with $200 as
the base case. For a 3.5-therapist clinic, that implies about $7,560 ARR
at the midpoint after annual discounting.

The unit economics are attractive if acquisition remains founder-led or
inbound: estimated CAC about $600, gross margin about 70%, and LTV:CAC
well above the threshold for a capital-efficient SaaS business. The
major sensitivity is price collapse. If the market only accepts $99 per
therapist per month, the business remains possible but loses much of its
premium vertical upside.

## Capital And Validation Plan

Validation should proceed in three steps:

1. Run a landing-page RAT for 2 weeks with $500 in targeted LinkedIn ads.
2. If the RAT passes, recruit 2-3 clinic-owner design partners within 90
   days.
3. Run paid concierge pilots using a BAA-covered workflow before building
   the full product.

The pre-build budget should stay under $3,000. If compliance setup costs
or timelines exceed that materially, the team should require a stronger
demand signal before continuing.

## Strategic Recommendation

Proceed only through validation. Do not build the product yet.

The first action is to publish a PT-specific landing page and run the
RAT with pre-committed thresholds: at least 5% ad-to-page click-through
from qualified traffic and at least 10% landing-page-to-email conversion.
Passing that test earns design-partner outreach. Failing it kills or
pivots the idea before expensive product work begins.
