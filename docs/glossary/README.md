# Business Glossary

Working vocabulary for business, startup, and product terms commonly used in
idea presentations, one-pagers, investor decks, and assessment reports.

Rule of thumb: spell out every acronym the first time you use it. Example:
`TAM (Total Addressable Market)`.

Definitions here are deliberately plain-language. The goal is not to win an
MBA argument — it is to explain an idea clearly and credibly.

## Market And Customer

- `TAM` (`Total Addressable Market`): The total theoretical market size if
  every potential customer bought your product. It is the ceiling, not the
  plan.
- `SAM` (`Serviceable Addressable Market`): The part of TAM you can actually
  serve with the solution, segment, and geography you work in.
- `SOM` (`Serviceable Obtainable Market`): The realistic share of the market
  you can win within a given period, typically 3-5 years.
- `Market size`: General term for how big the market is. Often used as an
  umbrella for TAM, SAM, and SOM.
- `Segment`: A bounded group of customers with shared needs or behavior.
- `Vertical`: A specific industry or niche — e.g. construction, healthcare,
  property management.
- `Vertical AI`: An AI-driven solution built for a single industry, with deep
  integration into that industry's data, regulation, terminology, and
  workflows. Stands in contrast to horizontal AI tools like ChatGPT or
  Copilot.
- `AI-native`: A product designed from the ground up with AI at its core,
  rather than bolted on top of an existing solution.
- `ICP` (`Ideal Customer Profile`): Description of the company or customer
  that best matches the product and is most likely to buy.
- `Persona`: A more concrete description of the individual user or
  decision-maker inside the customer's organization.
- `Buyer`: The person or role that can approve and pay.
- `User`: The person who actually uses the product day to day. User and
  buyer are often not the same person.
- `Decision-maker`: The person with authority to say yes or no.
- `Champion`: The internal person at the customer who pushes the solution
  forward.
- `Pain point`: A concrete problem the customer experiences as expensive,
  slow, annoying, or risky.
- `Problem severity`: How serious the problem is. A hard and expensive
  problem is easier to sell against than a mild irritation.
- `Hair on Fire`: Problem archetype where the customer has an urgent,
  obvious problem and is actively looking for a solution. Fastest path to
  revenue but the most competition.
- `Hard Fact`: Problem archetype where the customer has a real problem but
  has not recognized how serious it is. Requires an "aha moment" to
  activate.
- `Future Vision`: Problem archetype where the market or the behavior does
  not yet exist. Category creation. Hardest and slowest to validate.
- `Well vs crater`: Good startup ideas are narrow and deep (a well) — a
  small number of people who want the solution desperately. Bad ideas are
  broad and shallow (a crater) — many people who think "yeah, maybe".
- `Sitcom idea`: An idea that sounds plausible but that nobody urgently
  needs. Often generated by brainstorming rather than lived experience.
- `Schlep blindness`: The tendency to avoid ideas that involve messy,
  tedious work. But those ideas often have less competition precisely
  because everyone else avoids them.
- `Urgency`: How fast the customer needs a solution.
- `Market pull`: A sign that the market is already moving toward a solution
  — rising demand, new regulation, changing behavior.
- `Willingness to pay`: How much the customer will actually pay to have the
  problem solved.
- `Willingness to switch`: How willing the customer is to change their
  current behavior, tools, or vendor.
- `JTBD` (`Jobs To Be Done`): The task the customer is trying to get done,
  regardless of which tool they use today.
- `Use case`: The concrete situation where the product creates value.
- `Core workflow`: The primary professional workflow that defines a
  profession's identity and expertise — e.g. financial modeling for
  investment bankers, contract drafting for lawyers. Higher resistance to
  AI automation.
- `Supporting workflow`: Secondary or administrative tasks that support the
  core work — e.g. note-taking for doctors, marketing for dentists. Lower
  resistance to AI automation and faster adoption.

## Product, Value Proposition, And Wedge

- `Value proposition`: The short explanation of why someone should choose
  the product and what value they get.
- `USP` (`Unique Selling Proposition`): The specific sales argument that
  makes the offer sharp and memorable.
- `UVP` (`Unique Value Proposition`): A broader articulation of the unique
  value, not just a single sales argument.
- `MVP` (`Minimum Viable Product`): The smallest version of the product that
  can test an important assumption with real users.
- `Prototype`: An early model or demo that shows the idea, but is not
  necessarily production-ready.
- `Pilot`: A bounded engagement with a real customer to trial the solution
  in practice.
- `POC` (`Proof Of Concept`): A test that shows whether something is
  technically or operationally feasible.
- `Beachhead`: The first narrow market or first concrete customer type
  where you can realistically gain a foothold.
- `Wedge`: The narrow entry point that gets you into the market without
  having to solve everything on day one.
- `Entry point`: The first feature, first problem, or first customer group
  you use as your way in.
- `Workflow`: The work process the product is part of at the customer.
- `Tool vs agent`: The distinction between a product that helps humans do
  work (tool) and a product that does the work itself (agent). Agents
  replace entire tasks; tools support them. The difference affects pricing
  model, embedding, trust, and risk.
- `Delegation model`: Product design pattern where the professional user
  delegates work to the AI as they would to a competent junior colleague
  or an outsourced service provider. The user sets direction and reviews
  output; the AI does the work. Trust is built over time through accumulated
  evidence, not claimed up front. Every correction and approval is a
  feedback signal that improves the AI.
- `Embedding`: How deeply the product becomes part of the customer's daily
  work.
- `Switching costs`: What the customer loses — in time, data, learning, or
  risk — by switching away from the product.
- `Retention`: How well you keep customers or users over time.
- `Churn`: How many customers or how much revenue you lose over time.
- `PMF` (`Product-Market Fit`): When the product solves a real problem well
  enough to drive visible demand and repeat usage.
- `Design partner`: An early customer who helps shape the solution closely
  alongside you.
- `Lighthouse customer`: A credible reference customer who can be used as
  proof to the rest of the market.

## Go-To-Market, Sales, And Distribution

- `GTM` (`Go-To-Market`): The plan for how the product reaches its first
  customers and grows from there.
- `Distribution`: The actual path to customers — direct sales,
  partnerships, communities, or platforms.
- `Channel`: A channel you use to reach customers — resellers, integration
  partners, online advertising.
- `Inbound`: Customers find you through content, search, brand, or
  referrals.
- `Outbound`: You take the initiative through prospecting, cold email, or
  meeting booking.
- `PLG` (`Product-Led Growth`): Growth driven by the product's own
  adoption, often through a free trial or self-serve onboarding.
- `Sales-led growth`: Growth driven by active selling, demos,
  relationships, and negotiation.
- `Founder-led sales`: When the founder personally handles the first sales
  to learn the market quickly.
- `Partner sales`: Selling through a partner who already has the
  relationship with the customer.
- `Pipeline`: The potential deals moving through the sales process.
- `Funnel`: A stepwise model of how a potential customer moves from
  awareness to purchase.
- `Conversion rate`: The share that moves from one stage to the next — e.g.
  from demo to customer.
- `Sales cycle`: The time from first contact to signed contract.
- `Land-and-expand`: A strategy where you start small inside the customer
  and grow from there.
- `Expansion revenue`: Additional revenue from existing customers through
  more users, modules, or higher-tier plans.
- `CAC` (`Customer Acquisition Cost`): What it costs on average to acquire
  a new paying customer.
- `CAC payback`: How long it takes for the gross profit from a customer to
  repay the cost of acquiring them.
- `Top of funnel`: The wide part of the pipeline where many potential
  customers first enter.
- `Bottom of funnel`: The late part of the pipeline where customers are
  close to buying.

## Revenue, Unit Economics, And Finance

- `Pricing model`: How you charge — subscription, license, transaction,
  usage-based.
- `Outcome-based pricing`: A model where the customer pays per result
  (per claim, per contract, per report) rather than per user or per month.
  Natural for AI agents that do work a person used to do.
- `Customer type (elephants→flies)`: Classification of customers by annual
  value and realistic acquisition method: elephants ($100K+, field sales),
  deers ($10K+, inside sales), rabbits ($1K+, inbound), mice ($100+,
  virality), flies ($10+, ads/UGC). The pricing model must match the
  acquisition method the team can actually execute.
- `ARPU` (`Average Revenue Per User`): Average revenue per user.
- `ARPA` (`Average Revenue Per Account`): Average revenue per customer
  account.
- `ACV` (`Annual Contract Value`): The value of a customer contract on an
  annual basis.
- `MRR` (`Monthly Recurring Revenue`): Recurring monthly revenue.
- `ARR` (`Annual Recurring Revenue`): Recurring annual revenue.
- `Revenue quality`: How robust and attractive the revenue is. Recurring,
  high-margin, expansion-friendly revenue is typically better than one-off
  sales.
- `Gross margin`: Revenue minus direct delivery costs, measured as a
  percentage.
- `Contribution margin`: How much is left after variable costs to cover
  the rest of the business.
- `Unit economics`: The economics at the customer or transaction level.
  Shows whether the business makes sense when it scales.
- `LTV` (`Lifetime Value`): The total expected gross profit from a customer
  over the whole customer relationship.
- `LTV/CAC`: The ratio between a customer's lifetime value and the cost of
  acquiring them.
- `Burn rate`: How fast the company spends cash.
- `Runway`: How many months the company can continue at its current burn.
- `Breakeven`: The point where revenue covers costs.
- `Capital efficiency`: How much learning, traction, and revenue you get
  per unit of capital invested.
- `Cash conversion`: How fast revenue becomes actual cash on hand.
- `Working capital`: The capital needed to run daily operations — e.g.
  receivables, inventory, or payment terms.

## Strategy, Competition, And Moat

- `Moat`: A structural advantage that makes it hard for competitors to
  copy or overtake you.
- `Defensibility`: How likely the advantages are to hold up over time.
- `Competitive advantage`: The concrete advantage you have relative to
  others.
- `Incumbent`: An established player that already owns relationships,
  brand, or distribution in the market.
- `Substitute`: An alternative that solves the same need in a different
  way.
- `Direct competitor`: A competitor that closely resembles you in product,
  customer, and price.
- `Indirect competitor`: A competitor that solves the same problem through
  a different category or workflow.
- `Commodity trap`: When the product is so easy to copy that price and
  features become the only competition.
- `Data moat`: An advantage that arises because the product accumulates
  unique data or context over time.
- `Data flywheel`: A self-reinforcing cycle: the product does work →
  the work generates domain-specific data → the data improves the AI →
  the better AI does better work → more customers → more data. The moat
  is not the model, it is the dataset that only your product could have
  generated.
- `Structured feedback signal`: An automatic quality signal that tells
  whether the AI's output was good or bad, without manual review. Examples:
  did the candidate get hired? Did the lawyer edit the draft? Was the claim
  approved?
- `LLM wrapper`: A product that essentially sends user input to a
  foundation model API and returns the output with prompt engineering on
  top. No proprietary data, no feedback loop, no compounding advantage.
  A feature, not a business.
- `Network effects`: When the product becomes more valuable the more
  people use it.
- `Switching moat`: An advantage that arises because the customer loses
  too much by moving away.
- `Regulatory moat`: An advantage that arises because it is hard and
  expensive to obtain the necessary approvals, compliance processes, or
  trust levels.
- `Brand moat`: An advantage that arises because the market trusts you
  more than new entrants.
- `Operational complexity`: Complexity in regulation, procurement,
  onboarding, operations, or existing workflows. It is only good if you
  can turn it into a real advantage.
- `Right to win`: Why this particular team is better positioned than
  others to succeed.
- `Why now`: The concrete reason the timing is good right now.
- `Timing risk`: The risk that the market is too early, too late, or
  shifting in a way that damages the idea.
- `Platform risk`: The risk of depending on a single platform, API, or
  distribution channel.
- `Hallucination risk`: The risk that an AI model produces confidently
  wrong results. Severity depends on domain: embarrassing in marketing,
  costly in finance, dangerous in healthcare.
- `Model dependency`: The risk of depending on a single AI model provider.
  Price changes, deprecations, or policy shifts can break the product.
- `Horizontal AI exposure`: The risk that large horizontal AI providers
  (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic) add vertical features that make a
  specialized solution redundant.
- `Concentration risk`: The risk of depending on few customers, few
  partners, or few suppliers.

## Validation, Traction, And Decision

- `Validation`: The work of testing the most important assumptions before
  investing too much.
- `Assumption`: Something you believe is true but have not yet proven.
- `Hypothesis`: A testable assumption that can be investigated quickly and
  concretely.
- `Traction`: Signs of progress — paying customers, pilots, retention, or
  strong inbound interest.
- `Signal`: A data point that points to the idea being promising, but does
  not prove it on its own.
- `Leading indicator`: An early metric that points to future development
  — e.g. demo bookings or activation rate.
- `Lagging indicator`: A backward-looking metric that only moves later —
  e.g. ARR or annual result.
- `North Star Metric`: The single most important metric for the value the
  product creates.
- `Milestone`: A concrete sub-goal that shows progress toward a larger
  goal.
- `Decision memo`: A short document that gathers the conclusion,
  arguments, risks, and next steps.
- `Build now`: The conclusion that the idea is strong enough to pursue
  now.
- `Validate first`: The conclusion that the idea is promising but that
  one or two critical assumptions must be tested first.
- `Park`: The conclusion that the idea is interesting but not a priority
  right now.
- `No-go`: The conclusion that the idea should not be pursued in its
  current form.

## Quick Rules For Presentations

- Spell out acronyms the first time you use them.
- Use `SOM` in plans and budgets, not just `TAM`.
- Distinguish between `user`, `buyer`, and `decision-maker`.
- Distinguish between `wedge`, `beachhead`, and the full product vision.
- Do not use `moat` as decoration. Explain precisely what will be hard to
  copy.
- Do not confuse `complexity` with `advantage`. Complexity without edge is
  usually just friction.
